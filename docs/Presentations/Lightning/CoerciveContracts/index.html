In the privatized arbitration system, disputes involving corporations are diverted into a shadow process — where public accountability is replaced by predefined outcomes favoring the rich and powerful. These are the protections we are forced to surrender.

 
 

  • This is from a book contract, but it isn't just in publishing. The same mechanism is in nearly everything, even gym contracts.

  • The Corporate Intent unit was called in to investigate. I'm Detective Robert Golem, and this is from my case file.

Any dispute of any kind shall be submitted to binding arbitration before a single neutral arbitrator knowledgeable about the industries

I interviewed several witnesses for this investigation. First up: Cuyahoga Press — the repeat player, the side with experience. I asked for the Senior VP who signed the contract. They sent their Associate General Counsel.

 

Cuyahoga Press - Summary 1

  • She explained the process inc. how selection works, with both sides striking names from the AAA's list until 1 is left.

  • She minimized concerns about how often Cuyahoga goes to arbitration, the availability of arbitrators & the fairness of the process.

  • Corps use arbitration frequently

  • Individuals rarely use arbitration

  • Only a few arbitrators specialize in a field

  • Choice of arbitrators requires

    inside knowlege

Cuyahoga Press - Summary 2

She assured me that, despite the ubiquity of these clauses, they are not intended to be coercive. They are there to provide a faster, less expensive resolution to disputes for both sides, and that authors can always opt-out by simply chosing not to publish.

  • Binding arbitration clauses are

    standard in most contracts/industries.

  • Arbitration is effectively mandatory.

  • Individuals can often only choose to

    take it or leave it.

Cuyahoga Press - Summary 3
  • We then discussed who gets the benefit of the learnings from these processes.
  • She explained that both sides learn from all cases they are involved in, and that proceedings, including previous decisions by arbitrators, are kept confidential for the benefit of all parties.

  • Arbitration results often include a

    non-disclosure agreement.

  • No history and no precedent is created by

    these rulings. Each case is independent.

  • Only those involved in the case know the

    arbitrator, the arguments, and the outcome.

Cuyahoga Press - Summary 4
  • Finally, I asked why they felt the need to insist on these clauses.
  • The General Counsel explained that arbitration is an impartial process that is cheaper & faster than litigation. She said that both sides benefit, citing a 2022 report from the US Chamber of Commerce.

  • Company lawyers insist these clauses are necessary

    because, according to a 2022 Chamber of

    Commerce report, they:

    • Reduce costs by avoiding court
    • Result in faster resolutions
    • Maintain relationships by keeping conflicts private
    • Improve outcomes for individuals
  • I'd heard the company line and wanted an individual's perspective.
  • Since most cases are under non-disclosure, I reached out to an advocate who has worked with many in this system, Dr. Marcus Hale Senior Policy Analyst for the Midwest Center for Fair Contracting.
 

Individual Advocate - Summary 1

Despite the Chamber of Commerce's protestations, there is 30 years of evidence out of many prestigious institutions that the repeat-player advantage is real and that this private process favors businesses over individuals.

  • 30 years of studies

    • Institutions like Columbia, Cornell & legal journals
    • Repeat players win more often
    • Repeat players get better settlement terms

  • The Chamber of Commerce Study?

    • Doesn't stand up to scrutiny
    • Example: pressured settlements are treated as wins for individuals

Individual Advocate - Summary 2

Dr. Hale made it clear that there was no real choice -- That binding arbitration clauses are usually Adhesion Clauses, where individuals had to accept these terms or walk-away due to the power imbalance.

  • These are Adhesion Clauses
    • Individuals rarely can negotiate these clauses
    • Usually, no clause = no deal
    • The text is boilerplate, used everywhere
    • People sign because the either do so, or walk away

Individual Advocate - Summary 3

He explained that the lack of a public history means that only those that have participated in previous arbitrations have any idea about the process, the precedent, and the arbitrators. Companies know who has ruled in their favor in the past. Individuals have no way of knowing this information.

  • Individuals are locked-out of past decisions
    • Non-corporate entities can't learn from past cases
  • Repeat players build internal history
    • Companies know who & what worked for them

Individual Advocate - Summary 4

The evidence is clear: arbitration protects companies at the expense of individuals, and that individuals have no real choice in agreeing to these terms.

  • Corps say arbitration is faster, cheaper and better for all
  • Evidence shows that arbitration is unfair to individuals
  • It is efficient -- for the companies it protects
  • I’d finished the interviews. The evidence was solid. The pattern was clear.
  • Now it was time to bring the case to someone who could do more than talk. District Attorney Claire Kincaid had seen cases like this before.
 

Bringing the Case to the DA
  • I told DA Kincaid what I'd found: that once you're in arbitration, the game is over. She knew people were being funneled into a simulation of justice. She told me: "You're not asking me to prosecute a company. You're asking me to prosecute the system that protects them." -- She wasn't wrong.

  • Golem: This isn't the usual kind of case I bring you.

    Not a fraud ring. Not a bad actor we can cuff.

    This one is different. Once you're in arbitration,

    the game is rigged before you even sit down.

  • Kincaid: You're not asking me to prosecute a company.

    You're asking me to prosecute the system that protects them.

DA Kincaid on Adhesion
  • Kincaid said this survives on 1 fiction, that people agreed to it. But these clauses aren't negotiated. Most don't even know until after they're already committed. Courts call it consent, but she called it take-it-or-leave-it disguised as a choice. And once in, it's like your lawyer negotiated every word.

  • The courts call it consent, but it's really

    a requirement dressed-up as an agreement.

    A take-it-or-leave-it deal disguised as

    a choice. And once you sign the system treats

    it like you walked in with a lawyer and

    negotiated every word.

DA Kincaid on the Public Record
  • The DA then told me arbitration doesn’t just hide the outcome, it hides everything. From the moment that clause is signed, everything moves into the dark with no way for anyone else to know what happened. That darkness keeps the system lopsided. We walk in blind, but not the companies.
  • Everything in arbitration happens in the dark.

Not just the outcome — the entire process.

No transcript, no public record, no way for anyone

outside of that room to know what happened.

When every case is invisible, every injustice stays

isolated. That’s why nothing ever changes.

DA Kincaid on Constitutionality

  • Kincaid saved the hardest pill for last. She said that when people sign arbitration clauses buried in job offers, service agreements, and click-through screens, they're not just giving up a jury. They're giving up the constitutional right to petition the government for a redress of grievances. And the courts have said that's legal, because they treat the clause as consentual.

  • She told me the Supreme Court has been clear: if you sign a private agreement that waives your right to petition the government,
    the Constitution doesn't step in to save you. Yep, your First Amendment rights can be signed away with a checkbox. What do you think would happen if we tried that with our 2nd Amendment rights?

  • Congress has tried to fix this. The US House passed the FAIR Act in 2019 and again in 2022. Both times it went to the Senate Judiciary Committee and never even got a vote.

  • And that's when she looked at me and said, ‘You brought me a constitutional injury, Detective. But the law calls it a contract.'
    I walked out of her office knowing the truth: the system didn't steal people's rights. It asked for them — and the courts handed them over.

  • The right to petition the government is

    one of the oldest in the Constitution. But,

    if you sign an arbitration clause, even

    buried in boilerplate, the courts say

    you've waived that right. You can't go to

    court because the law treats that waiver

    as voluntary.

  • Congress has tried to fix this. The House

    passed the FAIR Act in 2019 and again in 2022.

    Both times it went to the Senate Judiciary

    Committee and never even got a vote.
 

  • Back at the office, I opened the file one last time. I noticed a few things I'd forgotten about. The pamphlet given to me by Dr. Hale for one. A few lines jumped out at me. They read:

  • "Some contracts include a small window—often 30 days—where you can opt out of the arbitration clause. These opt‑outs usually require sending a short letter by USPS mail to the company. If your contract offers this option, use it every time."

  • Practical advice. It also suggests opting-out even if the time window has passed, just in case. There was other practical advice too. A must read for anyone who has to sign a contract.

  • I thought about what Kincaid said — how Congress tried to fix this. How the House passed the FAIR Act in 2019 and again in 2022. And how both times the bill went to the Senate Judiciary Committee and never came back out. Not even an up-or-down vote. That's the part that stuck with me. Not the politics. The pattern:

    • A system that keeps disputes out of the public eye
    • A reform that never gets a public vote
    • A right that disappears in the fine print

  • My file didn't contain evidence of a crime. There was no smoking gun. It was a reminder — that some people are still trying to drag this into the light. And that the rest of us only see the truth when someone decides to look.

  • I have put the case file online in the hope that others will decide to look.

  • For now, the case remains open.
