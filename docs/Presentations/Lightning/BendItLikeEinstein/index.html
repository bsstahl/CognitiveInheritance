David Eagleman's Free-Fall Experiment

Neuroscientist David Eagleman has done some fascinating research about how we experience time. Some of this research involved flinging volunteers off of high towers and measuring if people accomplish more during free-fall then they can normally. It turns out falling off a tower is only the second most effective way to slow down time, the first, of course, is attending meetings! I mean, who needs high towers when you've got a two-hour status meeting?

Falling from the Tower: Created by Dalle via Microsoft Designer

  • Method:

    • Participants free-fall from a 150-foot tower
    • Attempt to read numbers on a "perceptual chronometer"

  • Hypothesis:

    • Heightened stress enhance abilities to perceive fast-flashing numbers
    • Would indicate a slow-down in time perception

  • Results:

    • Participants did not show improved number recognition
    • Suggesting Time slowing is a retrospective view
    • High-stress situations lead to increased memory encoding

Time Perception is Skewed by High-Gravity

As a Software Engineer I usually do the opposite. That is, I regularly make time fly. I mean, just give me a deadline and I'll watch it fly by. But really, when I'm building software I experience time very differently. Since I am focused on solving the problem, and not on experiencing the situation, my perception of time is skewed and it seems like the event happened faster than it did. This is why software engineers are notoriously bad at estimating the time their tasks take to complete. At least, that's the story I'm sticking with.

  • Focused Attention = Longer Perception

    • High-Stress (worry)
    • Manual Effort (calculation)

  • Autonomous Action = Shorter Perception

    • Low-Stress (flow)
    • Automatic Effort (habit)

This is why we suck at estimating!

The Paradox of Productivity

The key to understanding time-dilation then might be found in our attention spotlight. It seems as though we dedicate every waking moment to our mundane responsibilities, mainly because these tasks tend to monopolize our memory due to our constant worry over them. This perceived disproportion of time spent results in a feeling of underachievement, pressuring us to pour even more time into these tasks. This cycle squeezes out the time for activities we truly relish, creating a frustrating paradox of productivity. I remember flying through the air in slow motion - I don't remember flow-state at all. Time stopped during flow.

General Relativity: Created by Dalle via Microsoft Designer

  • Our mundane tasks monopolize our memory
  • This perception results in a feeling of underachievement
  • Which pressures us to spend even more time on them

Time flies in the zone, yet drags in detail; our minds make the mundane more memorable

Focus Modulation (FM)

That means, if instead of spending all of our energy focusing on making that leaping grab of a feature or diving catch of a bug-fix, we focus on being present in the moment for the things that make time seem to fly for us, we'll be bending our concept of time. Focus on Things that Make Time Fly: Created by Dalle via Microsoft Designer

  • Cultivate & document productive time

    • Setup the conditions for flow state
    • Limit interruptions and multi-tasking
    • Recognize and value flow when it ends
      • Narrate our work

  • Limit focus on less productive time

    • Reduce time spent worrying
    • Stratigic prioritization
      • Calendar the important stuff
    • Automate the boring stuff
