In the privatized arbitration system, disputes involving corporations are diverted into a shadow process — where public accountability is replaced by predefined outcomes favoring the rich and powerful.

These are the protections we are forced to surrender.

 
 
  • This clause is from a book contract, but don’t let that fool you. There’s nothing ‘publishing‑specific’ about it
  • The same structure shows up in employment agreements, credit card terms, software licenses, medical forms, even gym memberships
  • Different industries. Different contracts. Same mechanism.

Any dispute of any kind shall be submitted to binding arbitration before a single neutral arbitrator knowledgeable about the industries
  • Forced arbitration isn’t about this contract, it’s about the architecture behind all of them.
  • One arbitrator - No judge, no jury, no appeal, no public record, no precedent.
  • That’s not a dispute‑resolution clause. That’s a privatized justice system.
 
  • In the course of my investigation, I interviewed a number of witnesses familiar with the facts of the situation.
  • Let’s start with the repeat player in this case, Cuyahoga Press. I asked to speak with the Senior Vice President of Contract Administration whose signature is on the contract. Instead, they sent their Associate General Counsel that handles their dispute‑resolution matters.
 

General Counsel - Questions 1
  • She explained the arbitration process to me. How the selection of the arbitrator works, with both sides striking names until an agreement is reached.
  • She also explained how much more often Cuyahoga Press goes to arbitration versus how often their writers do.

  • Q: How often do publishers use arbitration?

    • A: Dozens of times a year.

  • Q: How often do authors use arbitration?

    • A: Most never do, If they do, it’s once.

  • Q: How are abitrators chosen?

    • A: AAA provides a list to both sides, they:
      1. Strike names they don't want
      2. Repeat until they agree

General Counsel - Questions 2
  • We then moved on to questions about the contracts themselves.
  • She assured me that the clauses are not intended to be coercive. That they are there to provide a faster, less expensive resolution to disputes for both sides, and that authors can always opt-out by chosing not to publish.

  • Q: What if the author doesn't agree with this clause?

    • A: These clauses are standard in all our contracts.

  • Q: What about other publishers?

    • A: Most companies have similar, even other industries.

  • Q: So they are effectively mandatory? There is no choice?

    • A: Authors can choose to publish or not.

General Counsel - Questions 3
  • We then discussed who gets the benefit of the learnings from these processes.
  • She explained that both sides learn from all cases they are involved in, and that proceedings, including previous decisions by arbitrators, are kept confidential for the benefit of all parties.

  • Q: How do I find out the result of an arbitration?

    • A: These are usually under non-disclosure.

  • Q: So no precident is set by these rulings?

    • A: Correct. Each case is independent.

  • Q: We can't see how arbitrators have ruled previously?

    • A: Right. This protects both sides.

  • Q: But you see other cases your company is involved in?

    • A: Yes, just as authors know cases they're in.

General Counsel - Questions 4
  • Finally, I asked why they continue to insist on these clauses.
  • Counsel explained their position that arbitration is an impartial process that is cheaper, faster, and more efficient than court litigation, and that both sides benefit from these advantages.

  • Q: Why do you feel these clauses are necessary?

    • A: These clauses protect everyone. They:
      • Guarantee an impartial process
      • Reduce costs by avoiding lengthy court battles
      • Provide a faster resolution of disputes
      • Maintain relationships by keeping conflicts private
      • Often improve outcomes over litigation for individuals

  • Q: Outcomes are better for individuals?

    • A: Yes, it's scientifically proven.

      See the Chamber of Commerce's 2022 Study.
  • Having heard the company line, I wanted an individual's perspective.
  • Since most of these cases are settled under non-disclosure, I reached out to a consumer rights advocate who has worked with many individuals who have faced binding arbitration proceedings, Dr. Marcus Hale Senior Policy Analyst for the MCFC (Midwest Center for Fair Contracting).
 

Individual Advocate - Questions 1

It was obvious from our conversation that, despite the Chamber of Commerce's protestations, and despite many arbitrators best-efforts, there is 30 years of scientific evidence out of many prestigious institutions that the repeat-player advantage is real and that this private process favors businesses over individuals.

  • Q: What happens when one side is a repeat player?

    • A: They win more often and with better terms.

      Arbitrators want to make a living too.

  • Q: What if the company isn't a repeat player?

    • A: The advantage shrinks — but doesn't disappear.

  • Q: How do you know all this?

    • A: 30 years of studies from Columbia, Cornell,

    Indiana and other Universities and legal journals.

  • Q: What about the Chamber of Commerce study?

    • A: Among many problems, it counts pressured

      settlements as wins for individuals

Individual Advocate - Questions 2

Dr. Hale made it clear that there was no real choice -- That binding arbitration clauses are usually Adhesion Clauses, where individuals had to accept these terms or walk-away due to the power imbalance.

  • Q: When do individuals get to negotiate these clauses?

    • A: Almost never, it's take-it-or-leave-it.

  • Q: What happens if they try to opt out?

    • A: The deal disappears, no clause, no contract.

  • Q: Do industries offer meaningful alternatives?

    • A: Most industries use similar boilerplate.

    That makes the contract a classic Adhesion

    All leverage is on one side.

  • Q: How do people handle this "choice"?

    • A: They sign because the only option

    they have is to walk away.

Individual Advocate - Questions 3

He explained that the lack of a public history means that only those that have participated in previous arbitrations have any idea about the process, the precedent, and the arbitrators. Companies know who has ruled in their favor in the past. Individuals have no way of knowing this information.

  • Q: What do individuals know of past decisions?

    • A: Usually nothing — the records are sealed.

  • Q: Can they see how similar cases were handled?

    • A: No — there's no public archive.

    Even California only captures metadata.

  • Q: Do repeat players have more information?

    • A: Yes — they build internal history over many cases.

  • Q: What does this do to fairness?

    • A: Individuals can't learn from past cases,

    only repeat-players can.

Individual Advocate - Questions 4

According to Dr. Hale, the evidence is clear: arbitration is used to protect powerful companies at the expense of individuals, and that individuals have no real choice in agreeing to these terms.

  • Q: What does the industry say about arbitration?

    • A: It's faster, cheaper, and better for individuals.

  • Q: Does the data support their claims?

    • A: No, overwhelming evidence shows

    that arbitration favors companies.

  • Q: Do individuals feel it saved them time or money?

    • A: Rarely — most say it drained both.

  • Q: Why this gap between the claim and reality?

    • A: The system is efficient,

    but only for the companies it is protecting.
  • I’d finished the interviews. The evidence was solid. The pattern was clear.
  • Now it was time to bring the case to someone who could do more than talk. District Attorney Kincaid had seen cases like this before — but this one felt different
 

Bringing the Case to the DA
  • District Attorney Kincaid didn't even look up at first — just tapped the corner of her desk, the way she does when she knows a detective isn't here for small talk. I told her this wasn't the usual kind of case I bring her. I laid the file on her desk — The witness statements, the thirty years of research. And I told her what I'd found: that once you're in arbitration, the game is rigged before you even sit down.
  • She looked up at me. Not annoyed — just tired. The kind of tired you get from seeing the same story play out a hundred times, and knowing how it ends. I could tell she understood the pattern. She'd seen it before.
  • But this time, she could see the structure behind it — the way the system funnels people into a process that looks like justice but isn't built to deliver it. That's when she said it. Not as a challenge — just as a fact: ‘You're not asking me to prosecute a company. You're asking me to prosecute the system that protects them.'
  • She wasn't wrong.

  • Golem: This isn't the usual kind of case I bring you.

    Not a fraud ring. Not a bad actor we can cuff.

    This one is different. Once you're in arbitration,

    the game is rigged before you even sit down.

  • Kincaid: You're not asking me to prosecute a company.

    You're asking me to prosecute the system that protects them.
